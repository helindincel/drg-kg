Marie Skłodowska Curie was a Polish and naturalized-French physicist and chemist who conducted pioneering research on radioactivity. She was the first woman to win a Nobel Prize, the first person to win a Nobel Prize twice, and the only person to win Nobel Prizes in two scientific fields. Her husband Pierre Curie was a co-winner of her first Nobel Prize, making them the first married couple to win the award and launching the Curie family legacy of five Nobel Prizes. In 1906 she became the first woman professor at the University of Paris.

She was born Maria Salomea Skłodowska on 7 November 1867 in Warsaw, then part of the Russian Empire. Her parents, Władysław Skłodowski and Bronisława Boguska, were teachers involved in the Polish national uplift aimed at restoring Poland's independence. The family had lost property through patriotic uprisings, and Maria grew up in financial difficulty. Her mother died of tuberculosis when Maria was ten; her sister Zofia had died of typhus three years earlier. These losses, according to biographers, contributed to Maria's move away from Catholicism toward agnosticism.

Maria graduated from a Warsaw gymnasium in 1883 with a gold medal but, as a woman, could not enroll in a Polish university. She studied at the clandestine Flying University and later worked as a governess to fund her sister Bronisława's medical studies in Paris. In Szczuki she fell in love with Kazimierz Żorawski, a future mathematician, but his family rejected marriage to the penniless relative. During those years she continued self-education through correspondence, tutoring, and laboratory practice at the Museum of Industry and Agriculture in Warsaw under her cousin Józef Boguski, who had assisted Dmitri Mendeleev in Saint Petersburg. After working in that chemical laboratory, she left Poland for France in late 1891.

In Paris she studied physics, chemistry, and mathematics at the University of Paris, living in poverty in the Latin Quarter while tutoring to survive. She earned degrees in physics in 1893 and mathematics in 1894. That year she met Pierre Curie, an instructor at the City of Paris Industrial Physics and Chemistry Higher Educational Institution, through the Polish physicist Józef Wierusz-Kowalski. Their shared passion for science drew them together. Pierre proposed marriage; Maria initially hesitated because she hoped to work in Poland, but Kraków University denied her a position because she was a woman. Pierre offered to move with her to Poland even if it meant teaching French. A letter from Pierre convinced her to remain in Paris and pursue a doctorate.

They married on 26 July 1895 in Sceaux without a religious service. Marie wore a dark blue outfit that later served as her laboratory clothing. They enjoyed long bicycle trips and scientific collaboration. In 1896 Henri Becquerel discovered that uranium salts emitted penetrating rays spontaneously. Influenced by Becquerel's work and Wilhelm Röntgen's discovery of X-rays, Marie decided to study uranium radiation for her thesis.

Using an electrometer developed by Pierre and his brother Jacques, she found that the activity of uranium compounds depended only on the quantity of uranium present. She concluded that radiation came from the atom itself, not from molecular interactions. Studying pitchblende and chalcolite, she found both minerals more radioactive than their uranium content alone could explain, suggesting the presence of an unknown, far more active substance. By 1898 she showed thorium was also radioactive. In July 1898 the Curies announced polonium, named for Poland; in December they announced radium, from the Latin word for ray. They also coined the term radioactivity.

Isolating radium proved arduous. Pitchblende is chemically complex; radium resembles barium and occurs with it in the ore. Through years of differential crystallization, by 1902 they separated one-tenth of a gram of radium chloride from a ton of pitchblende. Marie isolated pure radium metal in 1910 but never isolated polonium, which has a half-life of only 138 days. Between 1898 and 1902 the couple published thirty-two papers, including work suggesting radium destroyed tumor cells faster than healthy ones. Pierre increasingly devoted himself to Marie's research program. A new radium industry began developing, but the Curies did not patent their discoveries and benefited little from the commercial boom. In June 1903 the Royal Institution in London invited them to lecture on radioactivity; as a woman, Marie was not permitted to speak and Pierre delivered the address alone.

In June 1903 Marie received her doctorate under Gabriel Lippmann. That December the Royal Swedish Academy awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics to Pierre Curie, Marie Curie, and Henri Becquerel. Originally only the men were nominated; after Pierre's protest, Marie's name was added. She was the first woman to win a Nobel Prize. The Curies declined to attend the ceremony immediately, finally traveling to Stockholm in 1905. The prize money allowed them to hire their first laboratory assistant. The University of Paris gave Pierre a professorship and promised a new laboratory, though it was not ready until 1906.

On 19 April 1906 Pierre Curie died instantly when a horse-drawn vehicle struck him on the Rue Dauphine in heavy rain. Marie was devastated. In May 1906 the University of Paris offered her Pierre's chair; she accepted, becoming the first woman professor at the university. She sought to build a laboratory worthy of his memory. In 1909 Pierre Paul Émile Roux of the Pasteur Institute proposed that she move her work there after the university delayed providing adequate facilities. The threat of her departure led to the creation of the Curie Pavilion, jointly supported by the Pasteur Institute and the University of Paris. In 1910 she isolated radium and helped define a standard of radioactive emissions later named the curie.

In 1911 the French Academy of Sciences failed by one or two votes to elect her; Édouard Branly was chosen instead. Xenophobic press attacks portrayed her as a foreigner and atheist. That year a scandal erupted over her affair with physicist Paul Langevin, a former student of Pierre and a married man estranged from his wife. An angry mob gathered at her home while she was abroad. Despite the scandal, the Royal Swedish Academy awarded her the 1911 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for discovering radium and polonium, isolating radium, and studying its compounds. She remains one of only two people to win Nobel Prizes in two fields.

The second Nobel Prize helped her secure French government support for the Radium Institute, opened in 1914 for research in chemistry, physics, and medicine. After accepting the prize she was hospitalized with depression and a kidney ailment and avoided public life for much of 1912.

During World War I Marie recognized that wounded soldiers needed rapid radiological diagnosis near the front. After studying radiology, anatomy, and automotive mechanics, she developed mobile radiography units known as petites Curies. She directed the Red Cross Radiology Service and installed twenty mobile units and two hundred field-hospital radiological installations in the first year of the war, assisted by her daughter Irène. She trained other women as aides. In 1915 she produced hollow needles containing radium emanation—later identified as radon—for sterilizing infected tissue, using radium from her one-gram supply. An estimated one million wounded soldiers were treated with her X-ray units. She donated her Nobel gold medals to the war effort, though the French National Bank refused them, and she bought war bonds with her prize money. She summarized her wartime work in Radiology in War (1920).

After the war the Institute produced four more Nobel laureates, including Irène Joliot-Curie and Frédéric Joliot-Curie. It became one of the world's major radioactivity laboratories alongside Ernest Rutherford's Cavendish Laboratory, the Vienna Radium Institute, and the Kaiser Wilhelm Institute for Chemistry. Marie toured the United States in 1921 to raise funds for radium research; President Warren G. Harding presented her with one gram of radium collected in the United States. The French government offered her the Legion of Honour, which she refused. In 1922 she became a fellow of the French Academy of Medicine. She wrote a biography of Pierre Curie in 1923 and received the Cameron Prize for Therapeutics from the University of Edinburgh in 1931. She served on the League of Nations International Committee on Intellectual Cooperation until 1934 alongside Albert Einstein and Hendrik Lorentz. In 1932 she founded the Radium Institute in Warsaw, headed by her sister Bronisława; it is now the Maria Skłodowska-Curie National Research Institute of Oncology. Polish institutions, a Paris metro station renamed in 2007, and a centenary mural on her Warsaw birthplace commemorate her dual Polish and French legacy.

Marie Curie visited Poland for the last time in early 1934. On 4 July 1934 she died at the Sancellemoz sanatorium in Passy from aplastic anemia linked to long-term radiation exposure. She had carried radioactive samples in her pocket and worked without modern shielding, including while serving as a battlefield radiologist. She was buried in Sceaux beside Pierre; in 1995 their remains were transferred to the Paris Panthéon, sealed in lead because of residual radioactivity. She was the first woman interred there on her own merits. Her papers from the 1890s remain too radioactive to handle without protection.

The Curies' work reshaped twentieth-century science. Radium's intense radioactivity forced physicists to reconsider the conservation of energy and enabled Ernest Rutherford's probes of atomic structure. Hospitals hoped radium would enable new cancer treatments. Cornell historian L. Pearce Williams later wrote that the Curies' discoveries were epoch-making because radium's radioactivity could not be ignored and compelled a reconsideration of physics itself. Marie's daughter Irène won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry; her younger daughter Ève wrote a bestselling biography and became a journalist and humanitarian whose husband Henry Richardson Labouisse Jr. accepted the 1965 Nobel Peace Prize on behalf of UNICEF. Institutions in Paris, Warsaw, and Lublin bear the Curie name. Marie was known for honesty and modesty: she returned a scholarship once she could support herself, gave much of her Nobel money to colleagues and institutions, and deliberately did not patent the radium-isolation process so research could continue freely. Albert Einstein reportedly said she was perhaps the only person fame could not corrupt.
