Anthropic, PBC is an American artificial intelligence company headquartered at 500 Howard Street in San Francisco, California. The privately held firm develops a series of large language models named Claude and emphasizes AI safety as a core mission. It was founded on January 26, 2021, by seven former OpenAI employees, including siblings Daniela Amodei and Dario Amodei, who serve as president and chief executive officer respectively. Other founders include Jared Kaplan, Jack Clark, Chris Olah, Ben Mann, Sam McCandlish, and Tom Brown. By May 2026 the company employed roughly 2,500 people, Alphabet Inc. held a 14 percent ownership stake, and analysts estimated its valuation at $965 billion—making it, at that point, the most valuable pure-play AI company in the world. Anthropic planned to go public, filing confidential IPO papers with the United States Securities and Exchange Commission in June 2026.

Dario Amodei had been OpenAI's Vice President of Research before leaving with colleagues who disagreed with the parent company's direction. The Amodei siblings were central among those who departed. Anthropic raised $124 million in May 2021. In the summer of 2022 it finished training the first version of Claude but delayed public release, citing internal safety testing and a wish to avoid accelerating a hazardous race toward ever more powerful AI systems. Claude reached users in March 2023.

In 2024 Anthropic recruited several prominent figures from OpenAI, including alignment researcher Jan Leike and John Schulman. The following year brought a rapid expansion of product and infrastructure. In May 2025 the company announced Claude 4, introduced API capabilities including a Model Context Protocol connector, hosted its inaugural developer conference, and launched a web search API allowing Claude to access real-time internet information. Claude Code, Anthropic's coding assistant, moved from research preview to general availability. In September 2025 Anthropic said it would stop selling products to groups majority-owned by Chinese, Russian, Iranian, or North Korean entities, citing national security concerns. An October cloud partnership with Google granted access to up to one million Tensor Processing Units, potentially adding more than one gigawatt of AI compute capacity by 2026. In November Nvidia and Microsoft were expected to invest up to $15 billion in Anthropic while the company committed to purchase $30 billion of computing capacity from Microsoft Azure running on Nvidia systems.

That same November Anthropic reported that hackers sponsored by the Chinese government had used Claude to conduct automated cyberattacks against roughly thirty global organizations, bypassing safeguards by posing as defensive testers. In December 2025 Anthropic acquired Bun to improve Claude Code's speed and stability, and signed a multi-year $200 million partnership with Snowflake Inc. to distribute Claude models through Snowflake's platform. February 2026 brought a Super Bowl advertising campaign called "A Time and a Place," created by Mother, in which fictional AI assistants abruptly pivot to product pitches—Anthropic contrasted this satire with its pledge to keep Claude ad-free while competitor OpenAI introduced advertising on the free tier of ChatGPT. The company also donated $20 million to Public First Action, a nonprofit supporting political candidates favoring AI regulation.

A sharper conflict emerged with the United States government. Anthropic refused the Department of Defense's demand to remove contractual restrictions on using its technology for domestic surveillance and fully autonomous weapons. After that refusal the Pentagon designated Anthropic a supply chain risk, barring military contractors and partners from doing business with the firm. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth threatened in February 2026 to cut Anthropic from the defense supply chain unless it permitted unrestricted military use of Claude; Anthropic rejected the demand. President Donald Trump then ordered federal agencies to stop using Anthropic models. Reports indicated the U.S. military had nonetheless employed Claude via Palantir's Maven Smart System during operations in Venezuela and at the outset of the 2026 Iran war. The Pentagon investigated whether Claude played a role in a U.S. strike on an Iranian girls' school that killed more than 170 people, mostly children. On March 5, 2026, the Pentagon formally deemed Anthropic a supply chain risk; the company said it had no choice but to challenge the designation in court. Leaders from Google, Amazon, Apple, and Microsoft later backed Anthropic's lawsuit. A federal judge issued a temporary injunction in March 2026, writing that the Pentagon's actions appeared to be "classic First Amendment retaliation."

Anthropic operates as a public benefit corporation devoted, in its own description, to researching AI safety and reliability. In January 2026 it introduced a division called Labs, with Mike Krieger—formerly chief product officer and Instagram's co-founder—joining that effort. In March 2026 it launched the Anthropic Institute, a think tank on AI led by co-founder Jack Clark. Notable staff beyond the Amodei siblings include philosopher Amanda Askell, who works on Claude's character; security researcher Nicholas Carlini; chief science officer Jared Kaplan; alignment lead Jan Leike; interpretability co-founder Chris Olah; Andrej Karpathy, a former OpenAI co-founder and Tesla AI leader; and Nobel laureate John M. Jumper, who joined from DeepMind.

The company's flagship products are the Claude large language models, a name some employees associate with mathematician Claude Shannon. Models are fine-tuned using constitutional AI, training the system to follow a written constitution of principles. Claude is available through a web interface, an API, Amazon Bedrock, iOS and desktop applications for Mac and Windows, and specialized tools including Claude Code, Claude Cowork, Claude Design, and the Bun software runtime. Claude Code is a command-line agent for developers; Cowork offers a graphical interface aimed at non-technical users who want to automate file and task management without writing code. The rise of Claude Code helped popularize "vibe coding," a term coined by Andrej Karpathy in February 2025 for describing desired outcomes in natural language and letting an AI agent implement them.

Claude and Claude Instant debuted in March 2023 for approved users only. Claude 2 opened to the public in July 2023. March 2024 brought Claude 3 Opus, Sonnet, and Haiku in descending performance order; Claude 3.5 Sonnet followed in June 2024. May 2025 saw Claude 4 Opus and Sonnet; February 2026 brought Opus 4.6 and Sonnet 4.6; April 2026 added Mythos. National security reviews later led Anthropic to suspend foreign-national access to Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5 in June 2026 before partially restoring Mythos 5 for vetted partners.

Funding grew in successive waves. April 2022 financing totaled $580 million, including $500 million from FTX under Sam Bankman-Fried. Amazon became a minority investor in September 2023 with an initial $1.25 billion toward a planned $4 billion commitment, invested the remaining $2.75 billion in March 2024, and added another $4 billion in November 2024, making AWS Anthropic's primary cloud provider. Google invested $500 million in October 2023 with up to $1.5 billion committed over time and agreed to another $1 billion in March 2025. A March 2025 Series E round raised $3.5 billion at a $61.5 billion valuation led by Lightspeed Venture Partners. Series F in September 2025 raised $13 billion at $183 billion, co-led by Iconiq Capital, Fidelity, and Lightspeed. A term sheet in late 2025 pointed to $10 billion at $350 billion; Series G in February 2026 added $30 billion at $380 billion. The May 2026 round of $65 billion from investors including Altimeter Capital, Dragoneer Investment Group, and Sequoia Capital pushed the valuation to $965 billion.

Anthropic's government work placed it at the center of debates over military AI. In November 2024 it partnered with Palantir and Amazon Web Services to supply Claude to U.S. intelligence and defense agencies. A "Claude Gov" model announced in June 2025 was reported in use across multiple national security agencies; by February 2026 Anthropic's Palantir partnership made Claude the only AI model used in classified missions. In July 2025 the Pentagon awarded Anthropic a $200 million military AI contract alongside Google, OpenAI, and xAI. The Wall Street Journal reported Claude's use in the 2026 Venezuela raid to capture President Nicolás Maduro, an operation in which 83 people died, though exactly how the model was deployed remained unclear. Anthropic's usage policy prohibits direct use for domestic surveillance or fully autonomous weapons, restrictions White House officials said blocked FBI and Secret Service contractors from adopting the tool.

On the education front, Anthropic launched a Higher Education Advisory Board in August 2025 chaired by former Yale president and Coursera CEO Rick Levin, and partnered with Iceland's Ministry of Education and Children to give teachers access to Claude. A very different initiative, revealed in January 2026 court filings, was Project Panama: an internal effort described as seeking to "destructively scan all the books in the world." Anthropic purchased millions of used books from retailers such as Better World Books, sliced off spines, scanned pages to train Claude, and recycled the paper. Tom Turvey, who had helped build Google Books, was hired for the operation. Judge William Alsup ruled that destroying legally purchased books for training constituted fair use, unlike Anthropic's earlier reliance on pirated copies.

Research at Anthropic spans alignment, interpretability, and applied economics. Constitutional AI frames model behavior through a constitution—Claude 2's drew on sources including the 1948 Universal Declaration of Human Rights and Apple's terms of service, with rules such as choosing responses that "most support and encourage freedom, equality and a sense of brotherhood." The system evaluates outputs and adjusts models to better match those principles. Interpretability work studies neural "features," patterns of activation corresponding to concepts; in 2024 dictionary-learning techniques identified millions of features in Claude, including one linked to the Golden Gate Bridge. March 2025 research suggested multilingual models process meaning in a conceptual space before rendering language and that Claude sometimes plans ahead—for example selecting rhyming words before completing a poetic line. A September 2025 report found businesses mainly use Claude for full task automation rather than human-AI collaboration, echoing CEO Dario Amodei's warning that AI could eliminate many entry-level white-collar jobs in finance, law, and consulting.

Legal challenges accompanied rapid growth. In October 2023 music publishers including Concord, Universal, and ABKCO sued Anthropic for allegedly training on copyrighted lyrics, citing outputs reproducing songs such as Katy Perry's "Roar" and Gloria Gaynor's "I Will Survive"; Anthropic called the cited examples bugs. A August 2024 class-action by authors Kirk Wallace Johnson, Andrea Bartz, and Charles Graeber alleged training on pirated books. In June 2025 a federal judge ruled digital copies used for training were fair use but that millions of pirated library copies were not, sending damages questions to trial; Anthropic agreed in September 2025 to pay $1.5 billion—$3,000 per book plus interest—in what would be the largest U.S. copyright settlement if approved. In June 2025 Reddit sued for training on user data without consent, alleging breach of its user agreement.

By mid-2026 Anthropic embodied the tensions shaping the AI industry: extraordinary private-market valuation and product velocity set against safety commitments, copyright litigation, geopolitical export controls, and a public standoff with the Pentagon over how far a frontier lab would bend its principles for defense contracts. Its story—OpenAI alumni building a rival public benefit corporation, scaling Claude through Amazon and Google capital, and arguing in court that national security labels were retaliation for speech—offered a documented case study in how knowledge-graph extraction might capture organizations, people, products, funding rounds, partnerships, legal disputes, and policy positions from a single verifiable encyclopedic source.
